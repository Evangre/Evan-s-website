Fundamentals of Numeric Computing

Week 1

Materials Learned

  • Payroll and Taxes
  • Average and Weighted Mean

Payroll and taxes involve a number of calculations and operations that require numeric computing. For example, when calculating an employee's gross pay, you might need to multiply the employee's hourly wage by the number of hours worked, and then add any overtime pay or bonuses. To perform these calculations, you can use a programming language or spreadsheet software that supports numeric computing. This will allow you to write formulas or functions that can perform the calculations automatically, based on input data such as the employee's wage and hours worked. The average and weighted mean are two types of mathematical measures of central tendency that are used to summarize and describe a set of numeric data. Both measures provide a way to calculate a single value that represents the "typical" or "average" value in a data set, but they differ in how they take into account the different values and weights of the data points.

Week 2

Materials Learned

  • Real Income
  • CPI
  • Personal Income Tax

Real income is a measure of the purchasing power of a person's income, adjusted for changes in the price level. This measure is calculated by dividing the nominal income by the price level, and expressing the result in terms of a reference year or base year. The Consumer Price Index (CPI) is a measure of the average change over time in the prices paid by consumers for a basket of goods and services. This measure is used to track inflation and to adjust incomes, wages, and other economic data for the effects of inflation. Personal income tax is a type of tax that is levied on the income of individuals or households. This tax is typically calculated as a percentage of the taxable income, and it is used to fund government programs and services. To calculate personal income tax, you need to determine the taxable income, which is the amount of income that is subject to tax, after taking into account any deductions, exemptions, or credits that are allowed under the tax laws. The taxable income is then multiplied by the tax rate to calculate the tax liability.

Week 3

Materials Learned

  • Profit Analysis and Breakeven Point
  • Cost Volume Analysis
  • Constructing a Breakeven Chart

The breakeven point is the point at which the total revenues from a business activity or investment are equal to the total costs. This point is also known as the "break-even point" or "break-even point of sales." The breakeven point is calculated by dividing the total fixed costs by the difference between the price per unit and the variable cost per unit. This calculation shows the number of units that must be sold in order to break even, assuming that the prices and costs remain constant. Cost-volume analysis is a method of analyzing the relationships between costs, volume, and profitability in a business. This analysis is based on the assumption that costs have a fixed and a variable component, and that the total cost of a product or service is the sum of these two components. The fixed cost is the cost that does not change with the volume of production or sales, such as rent, salaries, or insurance. The variable cost is the cost that changes with the volume of production or sales, such as materials, labor, or shipping.

Week 4

Materials Learned

  • Simple Interest
  • Compound Interest

Simple interest is a type of interest that is calculated based only on the principal amount of a loan or investment, and not on any accumulated interest. This type of interest is typically used for short-term loans or investments, where the interest is paid or earned at regular intervals. To calculate simple interest, you need to know the principal amount, the interest rate, and the length of time for which the interest is being calculated. The interest is then calculated by multiplying the principal by the interest rate and the length of time, and expressing the result as a percentage of the principal. Compound interest is a type of interest that is calculated based on the principal amount of a loan or investment, and on any accumulated interest. This type of interest is typically used for long-term loans or investments, where the interest is compounded over time, meaning that it is added to the principal and becomes part of the new, larger principal that is used to calculate the next period's interest.
